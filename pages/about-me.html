Learning CS is the Future

Let me give you an example:
I wanted to build an LLM to run on a SpaceX probe—because space exploration is the future (and also, fish are going extinct—go watch that Netflix documentary, use your cognitive load after DOFO-ing all day).

Now, in that space machine, you'd need:

  • Highly optimized LLM infrastructure
  • Energy efficiency
  • Auto-recovery from failures

How would you do it? Let me guess:

  • "Oh, I'll DOFO it with a new JS framework!"
  • "Wait, no—a TS framework!"
  • "My bad, I'll use Rust… or Go…"
  • "Actually, screw it—C/Zig/C++ or even Assembly!"

And sure, there are YouTubers who'll tell you exactly how to scale with these languages. If not, there's always the "Build Your Own X" crowd—"Bro, you're a scientist! Be like John von Neumann!"

But then Jackson tells me: "This isn't real work. We just build frontends, backends, scale, deploy—sometimes CI/CD, sometimes direct. Your logic doesn't apply here."

And he's right. That's all most people do. AI automates it, and we do it super-fast. Just pay a subscription, burn some CPUs, heat the Earth, and boom—deployment ready.

One prompt → CPU + SIMD → Heat the Earth → Deployment.

So why learn hard CS? Because CS isn't just LeetCode. There are real discoveries to be made. Yes, dynamic programming is hard—but do you even know where you'd use it to build something real?
